Pacing notes

  • The first 30 minutes (Setup through Section 3) tend to take longer than expected because of setwd() problems and package installation issues. Budget extra time here.
  • contMap rendering (Section 5) takes 5–15 seconds depending on the machine. Warn students it is not frozen.
  • If you are running behind, Section 7 (trunk marbling) can be shortened to just showing the plot without discussion. Do NOT cut Section 8 — the independent work is where the learning sticks.

Common Student Issues and Solutions

“My tree won’t load” / “Error in file(file, ‘r’)”

Cause: setwd() path is wrong, or the data folder is not where they expect. Fix: Have them run getwd() to see where R thinks they are. Then check list.files("data/frederich/") to see if the files are visible. Most common issue: they are one directory level off.

“contMap is blank” / “Error: names of x are not in tree”

Cause: The names on the trait vector don’t match the tree tip labels. Fix: Have them check head(names(pc)) and head(tree_matched$tip.label). The names must match exactly. Most common cause: they forgot names(pc) <- tr$species or used the unmatched data frame instead of tr.

“K is NA” or “Error in phylosig”

Cause: Missing data (NA values) in the trait vector. Fix: Make sure they used na.rm = TRUE in the rowSums() call for pattern complexity. For individual traits, NAs should not be an issue since they are 0/1, but double-check with sum(is.na(my_trait)).

“What does K < 1 mean? Is that no signal?”

Cause: Confusion between significant signal and strong signal. Fix: K < 1 with p < 0.05 means there IS significant signal, but it is weaker than what you’d expect under pure Brownian motion. The tree predicts trait similarity, just not as strongly as a random-drift model would suggest. This is actually the most common result in nature — it means something like natural selection or convergence is partially overriding phylogenetic inertia.

“My K value is different from my neighbor’s”

Cause: The randomization test involves random permutations, so p-values will vary slightly between runs. Fix: This is expected. K itself should be identical (it is deterministic). Only the p-value changes. If they set a seed (set.seed(42)) before running, they will get identical results.

“install.packages doesn’t work” / “permission denied”

Cause: Lab machines sometimes have restricted write access. Fix: Try install.packages("phytools", lib = "~/R/library") and then library(phytools, lib.loc = "~/R/library"). Or install to a temp directory.

Key Teaching Moments

  1. The golden rule (Section 3): This is the most important concept for their final projects. Emphasize that every comparative analysis starts with matching tree tips to data. Students who skip this step in Part 2 will get cryptic errors. Spend time on this even if it feels tedious.

  2. K < 1 does not mean “no signal” (Section 6): This is the most common misinterpretation. Draw a quick diagram on the board: K = 0 (no signal, random with respect to tree) vs. K < 1 (some signal, weaker than BM) vs. K = 1 (matches BM exactly) vs. K > 1 (hyper-conserved). The p-value tells you whether signal is real; K tells you how strong it is.

  3. contMap is an estimate, not data (Section 5): The colors along internal branches are inferred ancestral values, not observed data. We only have data at the tips. Students sometimes treat the internal colors as facts. Remind them that ancestral state estimation involves assumptions (especially the BM model).

  4. Connection to Turing patterns (Section 7): The trunk marbling trait is a great bridge to Lecture 12. If marbling clusters in certain clades, it suggests the developmental toolkit for Turing patterns may be phylogenetically inherited, even if the specific pattern varies.

  5. “Phylogenetically conserved” requires nuance (Deliverable): Push students to avoid black-and-white answers. The correct answer to “Is color pattern diversity conserved?” is “It depends on the trait and the scale.” Pattern complexity shows weak but significant signal. Some individual traits may show strong signal. Others may show none. That’s biology.

Expected Results by Section

Section 1: Tree

  • 65 tips, root age ~65.3 Ma
  • Genera: Acanthurus, Ctenochaetus, Naso, Zebrasoma, Paracanthurus, Prionurus

Section 3: Matching

  • 62 species in common
  • 3 in tree but not in data
  • 21 in data but not in tree

Section 4: Complexity

  • Range: 4–19 pattern elements
  • Most complex: Acanthurus_bariene (19)
  • Simplest: Zebrasoma_rostratum (4)
  • Average by genus (approximate):
    • Acanthurus: n=26, mean ~10.2
    • Ctenochaetus: n=6, mean ~9.5
    • Naso: n=19, mean ~8.2
    • Zebrasoma: n=7, mean ~7.3
    • Paracanthurus: n=1 (single species)
    • Prionurus: n=3, mean ~6.7

Section 5: contMap

  • The fan tree should show warm (red) colors concentrated in the Acanthurus clade, with cooler (blue) colors in Zebrasoma and Prionurus. Naso is mixed. There should be visible shifts along some branches.

Section 6: Phylogenetic signal

  • Blomberg’s K = ~0.163, p = ~0.039 (significant)
  • Pagel’s lambda = ~0.294, p = ~0.019 (significant)
  • Both indicate weak but statistically significant phylogenetic signal. K well below 1 means the tree predicts complexity, but much less strongly than Brownian motion would predict.

Section 7: Trunk marbling

  • 17 of 62 species have trunk marbling
  • Concentrated in Acanthurus (11 of 26 species) and Ctenochaetus (3 of 6)
  • Absent in Zebrasoma (0/7), Prionurus (0/3), and Paracanthurus (0/1)
  • Visually clusters on the tree — not randomly distributed

“Your Turn” — Expected Results for Common Trait Choices

H_marbling (head marbling)

  • Present in ~12 species
  • Largely overlaps with trunk marbling (same Turing-pattern mechanism)
  • Moderate K, likely significant
  • Clusters in Acanthurus and Ctenochaetus

Ta_saddle (tail saddle marking)

  • Present in ~5–8 species
  • Scattered across clades — may NOT show strong signal
  • K likely non-significant (p > 0.05)
  • Good example of a trait that evolved independently multiple times

H_1_v_strip (single vertical head stripe)

  • Present in ~10–15 species
  • The classic “eye bar” — common across multiple genera
  • May show moderate signal
  • Good for discussing anti-predator function (eye camouflage)

Ta_colored_scalpel (colored caudal spine)

  • Present in ~10 species
  • The surgeonfish weapon — potentially an aposematic signal
  • Worth discussing: why would you advertise your weapon with color?
  • Signal strength variable

Ta_eyespot (tail eyespot)

  • Rare — present in only ~3–5 species
  • Low sample size makes signal tests unreliable
  • Good teaching moment about statistical power with rare traits

Tr_1_h_strip (single horizontal trunk stripe)

  • Present in ~12–18 species
  • Common in Acanthurus
  • Likely shows significant signal
  • Classic “lateral stripe” — discuss camouflage function (breaking up body outline)
