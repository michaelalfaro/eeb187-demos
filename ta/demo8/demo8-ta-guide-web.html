Demo 8 — TA Guide

Pavo + phylogeny + measurement error · ~75 min

This page is TA-only. Students don’t see it. It collects the concept scaffolds, expected outputs, timing, and recovery strategies for Wednesday May 20, 2026.

What today’s demo is actually about

Three questions, one per part, stacked:

  1. Is there structure in our continuous pattern data? (Part 1, ahistorical: PCA + bivariate lm().)
  2. Is that structure real biology, or is it just shared ancestry? (Part 2: phyloPCA + PGLS + K/λ.)
  3. Does the structure survive measurement noise? (Part 3: 5-image stack → per-variable SD → 100-iter Monte Carlo perturbation → CIs on K, λ, PGLS slopes.)

Each part also has a small ecology pendant (§1.8, §2.9) that pulls 4 FishBase traits (trophic level, max depth, max length, vulnerability) and asks: do pavo color-pattern axes track how the fish lives, before vs after phylogenetic correction? Part 2 §2.8 is a new walkthrough section that teaches students how to fetch those traits themselves via rfishbase — they reproduce the bundle’s species_traits.csv from species() + ecology() and then get a recipe for doing the same on their own family’s tip labels.

The pedagogical payoff students should walk away with:

Color patterns in butterflyfishes can change very fast, often faster than the ecological traits that supposedly drive them. So if you find low K for color, that’s a feature, not a failure of the analysis — it means convergence is rampant and you can’t read color off the phylogeny.

That sentence is your fallback if a student asks “what’s the punchline?” partway through Part 3.

Concept scaffolds — what you might need to explain

“Why is Blomberg’s K small for color patterns?”

K = 0 ⇒ trait is randomly distributed on the tree (close relatives look as different as distant ones). K = 1 ⇒ trait matches Brownian-motion expectation exactly. K > 1 ⇒ relatives more similar than BM predicts (rare; happens with strong niche conservatism). K = 0.1 (which Frédérich reports for Chaetodontidae) means color evolves much faster than Brownian drift would predict — i.e. there’s lots of convergence and reversal across the tree.

In our 19-species walkthrough, five of six pavo variables (m, Jc, Jt, m_dS, m_dL) come out at K between ~ 0.15 and ~ 0.45 with non-significant p. A is the exception — K ≈ 1.3, p ≈ 0.01. That’s K > 1, which is a stronger statement than “matches Brownian”; it means close relatives are more similar in pattern aspect ratio than drift would predict. Pattern orientation is actively conserved within butterflyfishes; everything else is labile.

“Why is A (aspect ratio) the only variable with strong K in our walkthrough?”

A = m_r / m_c measures the ratio of horizontal-vs-vertical pixel transitions, which directly reflects whether a fish is bar-y (vertical bars produce horizontal transitions) or stripe-y. Bar/stripe orientation is heavily constrained by body plan, fin development, and pigmentation-stripe genetics that have been documented to be highly conserved in reef fish lineages. It would be more surprising if pattern aspect ratio were not conserved.

The other five variables capture pattern complexity and boundary strength, which can change drastically with relatively minor pigmentation tweaks. That’s why convergence is rampant on those axes.

“Why use opponent-color distances instead of a full visual model?”

m_dS and m_dL need a coldists table. A full pavo workflow would compute coldists from a visual model (vismodel()) using receptor-noise-limited distances under an assumed visual system. We skip the visual model and just compute Euclidean distances on opponent-transformed RGBs:

  • col1 = (R - G) / (R + G) (red-green axis)
  • col2 = (G - B) / (G + B) (green-blue axis)
  • lum = R + G + B (achromatic)

This matches the recipe from Alfaro et al. 2019 (ICB), Demo 6’s simple_coldists(), and avoids the question of whose visual system the fish are signaling to. Pedagogically it lets students see what m_dS and m_dL measure without committing to a visual ecology assumption that needs its own lecture.

“Why mask the white background?”

FishView paints each fish onto a white field at export time. Without bkgID + exclude = "background", classify() will treat the white field as one of the 4 color classes — and adjacent() will count enormous numbers of pattern transitions at the fish/white boundary. That inflates m and dominates m_dS / m_dL. The pipeline picks the whitest class (highest R+G+B) and tells adjacent() to exclude it. Same recipe as Demo 6.

“What does the MC step in Part 3 actually do?”

For each of 100 (or 200) iterations:

  1. Pull a noise vector from N(0, error_sd) for each pavo variable.
  2. Add it to every tip’s value (every species gets the same noise SD applied independently).
  3. Re-run prcomp, phylosig(K), phylosig(lambda), pgls(A ~ m), pgls(m_dL ~ m_dS).
  4. Store the result.

The result is a 100-row × 6-column matrix of K values (and ditto for the other stats). The boxplot shows the spread; the observed-from-Part-2 value is the gold dot.

The diagnostic students should apply: if the observed dot is inside the box, your Part 2 conclusion is robust. If the dot is outside the box, sampling a different image would have given you a meaningfully different K — i.e. the Part 2 result was lucky.

For PGLS slopes (§3.6): the diagnostic is whether zero lies inside the MC 95 % CI. Not whether the observed slope lies inside (it does by construction).

Expected numbers from the Chaetodontidae walkthrough

Use these to spot-check student output mid-class. Numbers come from running the worksheet against the 19-species bundle (19 because Chaetodon_trifascialis got dropped — no tree tip).

Part 1

  • PCA: PC1 explains ≈ 42.5 %, PC2 ≈ 23.9 %, PC3 ≈ 14 %. Full-rank — no zero-variance trailing axes.
  • lm(A ~ m): slope close to zero, p typically > 0.3. Story: pattern complexity and aspect ratio are essentially decoupled in butterflyfishes.
  • lm(m_dL ~ m_dS): slope ≈ 0.05–0.10, R² typically 0.10–0.25. Chromatic and luminance boundary strengths are weakly positively correlated; many species have one without the other.

Part 1 ecology (§1.8)

  • lm(m ~ Troph): weak (R² < 0.1, p > 0.3). Trophic level doesn’t predict pattern complexity in this small sample.
  • lm(A ~ log_depth): also weak. Depth doesn’t predict directional asymmetry.
  • PC1 ↔︎ ecology correlations: all small (|r| < 0.3).

Part 2

  • phyl.pca with lambda = "ML" runs cleanly on the full 6-variable set (no rank-deficiency workaround needed).
  • pgls(A ~ m): slope near zero, often non-significant.
  • pgls(m_dL ~ m_dS): positive slope, p usually < 0.05 after phylogenetic correction.
  • Per-variable K (color): five of six variables (m, Jc, Jt, m_dS, m_dL) sit at K between ≈ 0.15 and 0.45 with non-significant p. A is the standout — K ≈ 1.3, p ≈ 0.01 (relatives MORE similar than Brownian, i.e. active conservation of pattern aspect ratio).

Part 2 ecology (§2.9)

Heads-up for the lecturer when slide 13 lands. The Chaetodontidae walkthrough does not show the “color evolves faster than ecology” pattern sharply — most ecology K values cluster near the color-pattern K values, not near K = 1. With only 19 tips that’s exactly what you’d expect; it’s a sample-size story, not a falsification of Frédérich. Frame slide 13 as “look for this in your own family — Chaetodontidae is too small,” not as “here is the punchline.”

  • K for ecology vars: Troph ≈ 0.33 (n.s.); log_depth ≈ 0.42 (n.s.); log_length ≈ 0.72 (p ≈ 0.02); Vulnerability ≈ 0.36 (n.s.).
  • Takeaway for the data we’ll show: body size is the one ecological trait with significant phylogenetic structure in this 19-species sample. All other ecology K’s overlap with the color-pattern K’s, so the comparison plot looks like one cloud, not two. Each student group’s own family (some with deeper trees and more species spread) is where the comparison may sharpen.

Part 3

  • Error-stack means (Heniochus_acuminatus): vary depending on bundle; check the printed table.
  • CV across the 5 images: lowest for A (the conserved variable — same fish, same aspect ratio across angles ≈ 0.05–0.15); higher for m_dS, m_dL (lighting-sensitive boundary strengths ≈ 0.3–0.6).
  • MC K distributions: centred near observed values; spread is widest for m_dS, m_dL. A’s K boxplot stays tight around 1.0–1.4 — its observed signal is robust.
  • MC PGLS slope for A ~ m: CI typically covers 0 → no real bivariate relationship survives noise.
  • MC PGLS slope for m_dL ~ m_dS: depends on how clean the boundary metrics come out; CI may or may not exclude zero.

Group-bundle FAQ — what each group ends up with

Each group exports a bundle from FishView during the in-class 15-min block. The shape is identical to the Chaetodontidae walkthrough bundle (tree, images, README, picked_*.txt manifests, species_traits.csv), but:

  • Group bundles will be smaller than 20. The picker requires ≥ 15 species across ≥ 5 genera. Most groups will pick 15–18, not 20. K estimates get noisier at lower N — flag this in the wrap-up if their K boxplots look wide.
  • Species lists differ. Don’t compare K values across families directly.
  • Trees have different ages/topologies. K and λ scales are interpretable within a family, not across families.
  • Error-species varies. Each group picks their own. The README names it; the worksheet’s auto-parse picks it up. If a student hard-codes Heniochus_acuminatus from the Chaetodontidae walkthrough, they’ll crash. Tell them to re-run §1.2 and §1.3 fresh after switching bundle.
  • Some species may drop with no tree tip. Just like Chaetodon_trifascialis did in the walkthrough. The worksheet’s name-prefix matching handles this; students just see a smaller length(species) than they picked.
  • species_traits.csv is generated server-side at Export time (FishBase fields: Troph, DepthRangeDeep, Length, Vulnerability). Some species have missing values for some traits — na.action = na.pass in §1.8 / §2.9 handles this. Don’t panic if a group’s signal_eco table has fewer rows than ecology variables. The §2.8 rfishbase walkthrough also shows students which traits are gappy for their family — flag this if a group plans to use one of those traits in a writeup claim.

Sample writeup the lecturer wants from each group

“Our family — — showed [strong / weak / moderate] phylogenetic signal in color-pattern axes (K range = [X–Y]; significant for [Z out of N] variables). Body size was the only ecological trait with significant signal. After phylogenetic correction, the bivariate <A ~ m> relationship [survived / disappeared]. Under realistic measurement error (CV ≈ [X] from our 5-image error-species), the signal in <variable> still / no longer survived. We conclude that color patterns in [match / contradict] Frédérich et al.’s headline finding of fast convergent evolution.”

If a student walks out with three sentences in this shape, the demo worked.

What to do if Wednesday goes sideways

  • FishView server down → no group picker: this is the most disruptive failure mode now that the in-class bundle build is on the critical path. Recovery plan: the Chaetodontidae walkthrough is pre-downloaded by every student in Step 3 of prep, so you can re-frame the lecture as “we’ll all run Parts 2 + 3 on Chaetodontidae together, and you’ll do the picker work as homework once the server is back.” Their writeup then compares Chaetodontidae to Frédérich rather than their family. Notify the class within the first 5 min if the server is unreachable.
  • Server up but slow / one group can’t export: other groups continue; that group falls back to the Chaetodontidae bundle for the in-class portion and finishes their family’s bundle as homework.
  • Wi-fi out → no FishView, no download.file: Part 3 doesn’t need the network once the bundle is unzipped, so anyone who downloaded the Chaetodontidae bundle in prep can still complete Parts 2 and 3 against it.
  • R session crashes on a student laptop: the if (!exists()) reload pattern at the top of Parts 2 and 3 means they can re-run a single chunk to recover. They lose Part 1’s plot images but not the trait matrix.
  • rfishbase fails to install on a group’s laptop: they can still run §2.9 onward — that section reads from the bundled CSV, not the live pull. Skip §2.8 for that group and tell them to use the CSV-based combined data directly. The downstream PGLS / K-on-ecology chunks don’t need rfishbase.

- **MC PGLS slope for `A ~ m`:** CI typically covers 0 → no real bivariate relationship survives noise.
- **MC PGLS slope for `m_dL ~ m_dS`:** depends on how clean the boundary metrics come out; CI may or may not exclude zero.

## Common student traps + how to recover

| Symptom | Cause | Fix |
|---|---|---|
| `Error in adjacent(cl, xpts = 50) : Required argument xscale is missing` | Old worksheet version cached | Have them re-download the bundle and the worksheet HTML; they have a stale chunk. |
| `phyl.pca` errors with "system is computationally singular" | Their bundle has fewer than 6 species with usable images (rare) OR they're running an older worksheet that still drops `p_4`/`m_c` from a 9-variable set | The new 6-variable set ({m, A, Jc, Jt, m_dS, m_dL}) is full-rank so this shouldn't happen — re-download the worksheet HTML. |
| `signal[, c("variable", "K")]` errors with "undefined columns" | They skipped chunk `signal-bar` which adds `signal$variable <- rownames(signal)` | Just run that line; it's a one-liner. |
| 5-image stack chunk shows `NULL` for `err` | They restarted R and didn't re-source `measurement-error.R` | The §3.1 reload block exists for this — make sure they ran it. |
| Monte Carlo hangs > 5 min | `n_iter = 200` and a slow laptop OR a `pgls` call hitting a tough λ optimum | Reduce to `n_iter = 50`. Quality of conclusions barely changes. |
| `read.csv("species_traits.csv")` fails with "file not found" | They unzipped the bundle in the wrong working dir | `getwd()` and `list.files(bundle)` will diagnose. Bundle should be in the working dir, *not* inside `data/`. |
| Their PCA shows wildly different proportions of variance than the worked example | They're on a different family bundle. Expected. | Remind them this *is* their group's headline result, not the Chaetodontidae one. |
| FishView Export button is greyed out | One of the picker's validation checks failed: < 15 species, < 5 genera, a starred species has no exemplar, no error-species marked, or error-species has < 5 images. | The picker now shows which check is failing as a banner. Walk them to the violation and fix it. |
| Group ends up with 18 *Chaetodon* and 2 other genera | They check-boxed the first 18 species without watching the genus counter | Push them to **uncheck** until they're down to ≤ 6 *Chaetodon* and pick 9+ species from other genera. The bundle has to span the family, not a clade within it. |
| All 5 error-species images look identical (same fish, same shot, slight crops) | They picked five thumbnails that are duplicates of the same photo | Tell them the *whole point* of the 5-image stack is to capture how a different photo of the *same species* changes pavo values. Same shot ≠ different photos. They need angle / lighting / photographer variation. |
| Group's K plot is mostly empty / 4 of 9 vars have no boxplot | Their bundle came in below 12 tips after tree-pruning. K estimation is unstable at that N. | Have them add 3–4 more species from FishView (Export again — yes, they can re-export) and re-run. |
| `bundle <-` is still pointing at `chaetodontidae-mini-bundle` after they exported their family's | They forgot step F of "Build your bundle" — switching the path | One-liner: `bundle <- "acanthuridae-mini-bundle"` (or whichever family). Re-run from §1.2. |

## Group-bundle FAQ — what each group ends up with

Each group exports a bundle from FishView during the in-class 15-min block. The shape is identical to the Chaetodontidae walkthrough bundle (tree, images, README, `picked_*.txt` manifests, `species_traits.csv`), but:

- **Group bundles will be smaller than 20.** The picker requires **≥ 15 species across ≥ 5 genera**. Most groups will pick 15–18, not 20. K estimates get noisier at lower N — flag this in the wrap-up if their K boxplots look wide.
- **Species lists differ.** Don't compare K values across families directly.
- **Trees have different ages/topologies.** K and λ scales are interpretable within a family, not across families.
- **Error-species varies.** Each group picks their own. The README names it; the worksheet's auto-parse picks it up. If a student hard-codes `Heniochus_acuminatus` from the Chaetodontidae walkthrough, they'll crash. Tell them to re-run §1.2 and §1.3 fresh after switching `bundle`.
- **Some species may drop with no tree tip.** Just like `Chaetodon_trifascialis` did in the walkthrough. The worksheet's name-prefix matching handles this; students just see a smaller `length(species)` than they picked.
- **`species_traits.csv` is generated server-side at Export time** (FishBase fields: Troph, DepthRangeDeep, Length, Vulnerability). Some species have missing values for some traits — `na.action = na.pass` in §1.8 / §2.9 handles this. Don't panic if a group's `signal_eco` table has fewer rows than ecology variables. The §2.8 rfishbase walkthrough also shows students *which* traits are gappy for their family — flag this if a group plans to use one of those traits in a writeup claim.

## What to do if Wednesday goes sideways

- **FishView server down → no group picker:** this is the most disruptive failure mode now that the in-class bundle build is on the critical path. Recovery plan: the Chaetodontidae walkthrough is pre-downloaded by every student in Step 3 of prep, so you can re-frame the lecture as *"we'll all run Parts 2 + 3 on Chaetodontidae together, and you'll do the picker work as homework once the server is back."* Their writeup then compares Chaetodontidae to Frédérich rather than their family. Notify the class within the first 5 min if the server is unreachable.
- **Server up but slow / one group can't export:** other groups continue; that group falls back to the Chaetodontidae bundle for the in-class portion and finishes their family's bundle as homework.
- **Wi-fi out → no FishView, no `download.file`:** Part 3 doesn't need the network once the bundle is unzipped, so anyone who downloaded the Chaetodontidae bundle in prep can still complete Parts 2 and 3 against it.
- **R session crashes on a student laptop:** the `if (!exists())` reload pattern at the top of Parts 2 and 3 means they can re-run a single chunk to recover. They lose Part 1's plot images but not the trait matrix.
- **rfishbase fails to install on a group's laptop:** they can still run §2.9 onward — that section reads from the bundled CSV, not the live pull. Skip §2.8 for that group and tell them to use the CSV-based `combined` data directly. The downstream PGLS / K-on-ecology chunks don't need rfishbase.
